From biophilia to ecophobia

 

Evidence suggests that humans are instinctively attracted to nature. Erich Fromm termed this connection biophilia, meaning the passionate love of life and all that is alive. (Fromm, 1992), or as Wilson stated, the urge to affiliate with other forms of life (Kellert & Wilson, 1995, p. 416). This indicates that a connection to nature is inherent to our identity. However, with increasing urbanisation and over half of the worlds population living in cities (World urbanization prospects: the 2011 revision), there are ever more barriers between us and nature. Our workplaces and homes are becoming sterile places, sheltered from nature (Schultz, 2002), with people are living videophilic lives fixated on screens, ecophobia (Louv, 2005) is becoming rife, and the fear of nature has become damaging to our health (Gray, 2013)

 

Humans' relationship to food is a fundamental basis for our connection to the environment (Kimmerer, 2013; Pretty, 2002); our cultures and social systems evolved (Marean, 2016) with a link to nature, and as food was considered a gift from nature, it was valued accordingly (Pretty, 2002). Due to the Industrialisation of food production, food is now considered a commodity (Hale et al., 2011), and regional identities spawned from connections to local food are disappearing, dehumanising people. This growing disconnection has allowed intensified agriculture, causing soil depletion, pollution, and biodiversity loss (OKane, 2012). Robin Wall Kimmerer noted that she was unable to experience a reflective moment for plastic, as it was so far removed from life, similar is our relationship with food; if we can no longer see its place in the environment, we can have no reciprocal relationship with it (Kimmerer, 2013, p.155).

 

The war in Ukraine and the resulting global food crisis (Nichols, 2022) highlight our reliance on the current worldwide industrial food system and how abstract our relationship to what we eat is. We do not know where it grows until it is at risk, its production becomes anonymous, and we expect all food in the shops year-round. Additionally, this shows how limited our diet is, dependent on a small number of staple crops. The Irish potato famine showed us how a homogenous system could prove disastrous (Kinealy, 1997). Self-provisioning, foraging, and eating seasonally are common throughout eastern Europe (Sovov et al., 2021, Pungas, 2019). In the Bosnian war, local connections to surroundings proved crucial (Redi, 2010) in subsidising limited food supplies. This shows how meaningful our relationship to the environment is socially and how it can provide resilience in our uncertain futures.

Journey

I grew up in suburbia, my connection to the environment has been a lifelong realisation. I was always fond of animals, as a teenager, one evening meal I had the simple epiphany that the duck on my plate was no different to a duck by a pond this was no small wonder, with my lack of farming experience. My main issue was not with killing animals, but their lifes purpose only to exist for us. Kimmerer commented on this process, Something is broken when the food comes on a Styrofoam tray wrapped in slippery plastic, a carcass of a being whose only chance at life was a cramped cage. That is not a gift of life; it is a theft. (Kimmerer, 2013). My parents dismissed my wish to become a vegetarian as a fad, meaning from this point, I also had to start preparing my own meals, initiating the continuing question of where my food comes from. We need to restore honor to the way we live, so that when we walk through the world, we dont have to avert our eyes with shame. (Kimmerer, 2013).

 

I have travelled often; this has allowed me to experience cultures and look back at the UK with reflection. I have spent much time in eastern Europe, where the free-market mentality has not yet entirely adulterated hospitality. Pensioners have invited me into their homes and shared an abundance of homegrown food; without monetary value, why would they not share the wealth? On my first visit to Bulgaria, I was constantly asked how I liked the nature; this surprised me, as I considered my British identity connected to artificial cities, buildings, and even our national parks are protected for their picturesque heritage, but anything but natural (Ritvo, 2015). I envied the pride they felt for their native land. After the fall of communism, people moved to the cities with the promise of work, but the young have started to move back to their country, disillusioned with the myth and seeing their grandparents live a better life (Budjeva, 2020). Could we learn from this internationally?

 

My journey continued through veganism and then permaculture, as the only way I could guarantee food was subject to my morals was to oversee its growth myself. I wished to broaden my positive effects on the natural world, leading me to study Ecology, but by my third year, I realised that I had become distracted from my original purpose by burying my head in academia. I considered ecological theory all well and good, but not practical if the human element is absent. The starkest realisation was during my study of two modules on agriculture in parallel; one was focused on how efficiently we could utilise natural resources entirely for our gain utterly incompatible with my beliefs, whilst the other was on our growing disconnect from nature and how to solve this. This reminded me of why I started this journey in the first place.

 

The word ecology and economy come from the same root, the Greek word Oikos, meaning home or household, but whereas the promotion of ecological principles could be considered reconnecting organisms to their home (the environment), the growth of economies could be considered the opposite. American Economic Association defines economy as the study of scarcity, the study of how people use resources and respond to incentives. Scarcity is the driving force, what follows is commodification, favouring disconnection, and discouraging abundance. This brings people to believe that it is money that we require to live rather than ecosystems. However, this is not the only model., Anthropologists have observed different views on providing for ourselves, including gift economies.

 

I hope to use this study to put into practice these different worldviews and as an opportunity to reconnect with my original goals on my journey in connecting with nature.

Self-provisioning

Over the past few years, I have established some botanical knowledge, including wild edibles, but have eaten little more than a few leaves whilst out for a walk. I have made no conscious effort to forage. In this experiment, I hope to integrate foraging as decoration for my diet, replacing store-bought salad with foraged or otherwise self-provisioned food.

Growing our food has created emotional connections with our environment (Foster, 2009). I had already experience in growing my food and was doing so on a limited scale at my term-time address; these raised beds with watercress, wild rocket and other greens could act as a fallback if foraging did not go to plan. One thing that led me on my food journey was the idea of control. I decide what goes in and what comes out, primarily within my timeframe. It is a case of what it can give me, rather than what can I learn from it, whilst foraging is stepping into an ecosystem, abiding by where, how, and when things grow, dependent on the qualities of the environment you are in it is this connection I hoped to gain.

 

Library

There are ~400,000 species of plants, and at least half of these are edible for humans. Yet, we currently eat only around 200 of these species, with only three crops maize, rice, and wheat accounting for half of the worlds populations calories (Wong, 2020). This is remarkable as our species is considered a classic generalist specialist, able to adapt to different food webs this is how we managed to populate the earth and grow our knowledge of our environment and the food it could provide through ingenious ways (Anderson et al., 2012).

This abundance of plants could be thought of as a library, not only for knowledge but nutrition and medicine, and were only taking out a handful of books. Suppose we do not take care of and utilise this bank of opportunities and limit ourselves to a few species. In that case, we may put ourselves at their mercy.

The industrial food system has encouraged ecophobia, raising us on the belief that only bought food is safe, but with pesticides used within my life now being banned. I would encourage more trust in the food in the UKs wild foods that have been eaten for generations without issues. Elsewhere, wild edible plants are still consumed by a large percentage of the global population, often a fundamental part of food security (Ray et al., 2020).

 

Names

A crucial part of our relationship with the world, which is mostly unique to our species, is symbolic thought (Carlson & Zelazo, 2008, pp. 288-297). To develop a relationship, one must know the names of species, names are the way we humans build relationship (Kimmerer, 2013, p.208). Our knowledge of our environment has been shaped by what we know, and for many of us now, that is artificial. Indeed, my young nephew is proficient in naming all prominent car manufacturers, but many could not identify the weed in the crack of a pavement.

Once a plant is identified, what it looks like, and where it grows can be established. I noticed this was a growing connection to each species throughout the season; for example, as the wild garlic died back, I instinctively knew where I would still find it, in the shade across the stream. Kimmerer had a similar experience when the strawberries taught her how they spread by runners' (Kimmerer, 2013).

 

Grammar of animacy

English is noun based, with 30% verbs. Whilst Potawatomi, a native American language, is 70% verbs (Kimmerer, 2017, pp. 128-134). Verbs are animate; a world where everything is a verb is a world where everything is alive. Could we learn from this? If Garlic mustard was Jack by the Hedge, would we think twice about spraying pesticide on him rather than on it?

We do not hesitate to give a gender to a car or a boat, anthropomorphising it to the same level of respect as ourselves, but not to a carrot we feel no obligation. Turning something alive into a noun makes it a resource, with no care of where it came from.

Honourable Harvest

I successfully replaced store-bought salad with foraged salad (Figure 1). In practice, the process was easy - walking from my home to the field whilst taking my dog for a walk; there was something edible along every roadside. Half the work was done as I was able to identify species which left me with time to think about the meaning of their names, where they are, how long, and why. Consciousness was critical in giving meaning to the process. Much like Kimmerers experience in the Honourable harvest (Kimmerer, 2013), the juxtaposition was notable. Daily I walked through non-diverse perennial ryegrass fields, to forage on the abundant woodland edges, a constant reminder of how fortunate I was to know of these spots.

I questioned why I had not done this already. I concluded that it was not due to time constraints; it was more likely cultural insecurity, worried about what others may think of seeing me picking at hedges. Living by other peoples views on what is proper food or not. This meant that I was not only questioning my relationship with the environment directly but my relationship with society.

My research into the seasonality and connection to land introduced me to a Ukrainian national soup made with nettle, sorrel, and wild garlic (Morgan, 2022). I looked at this not just as a recipe but to experience part of Ukrainian identity. This brought me not only a greater connection to food but also global issues, creating a commonality with the people on the television reminding me that we share the same earth.

 

Belly of my brother

A neighbour of mine forages and often offers me mushrooms. With an abundance, it is no extra cost for him. The currency in a gift economy is relationship (Kimmerer, 2010). Similarly, I let him pick the rocket from my raised bed. The exchanges of gifts are of mutual benefit through continued reciprocity.

I used to be an avid hitchhiker, as I consider the only way to truly know a country is through its people. If you are not paying for the experience, it could be nothing but genuine. This act was reciprocatory. Whereas a bus driver lets their passengers on due a monetary incentive and is obliged, a ride shares their abundance at no cost to them through choice, both parties benefitting from a chat. When purchasing an item from a shop, there is no inherent obligation, as it becomes private property. But the association with a gift is often more valuable; a relationship of gratitude and a bond has been created.

 

Throughout my foraging, I utilised freely available online data extensively to validate plants for edibility; this and the free software movement are consistent with gift economy ideas, showing that despite human divergence from the natural world occurring parallel to technology, the act of reciprocity can still exist in this age, even digitally.

 

Conclusion

This experiment brought me personally closer to nature daily. Rather than falling back on the store-bought salad disconnected from its source, I plan to continue foraging as a daily routine. It has reconnected me to my surroundings and changed the act of procuring my lunch to an extended conscious connection rather than a fleeting decision. My association with each species extends throughout the seasons, and I am more open to reading what the plants may be telling me. Removing instant gratification creates a relationship, like the sowing of a seed. Following a patch of wild garlic throughout the season has brought gratitude and connection to its life. The continued edible discoveries help me continue to be excited and proud of the biodiversity and wonders of my surroundings.

 

I have demonstrated that those who feel unsatisfied with the industrial agriculture system could benefit from self-food-provisioning, either through growing their food or, as I have shown through, how easy it is to find an abundance of wild edibles. For those not fortunate to understand the detrimental effects of the food systems they take part in, education is essential (Pretty, 2002).

 

Foraging has proven more than decorative and could provide profound changes:

 

       Foraging diversifies the food supply Providing resilience.

 

       Foraging localises the food supply Reconnecting.

 

       Foraging builds community Relationships as a currency.
